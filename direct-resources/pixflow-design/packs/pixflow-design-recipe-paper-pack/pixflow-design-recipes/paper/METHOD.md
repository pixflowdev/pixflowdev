# Paper Recipe

Paper is a light document-workspace method for products that organize writing, knowledge, collaboration, notes, specs, templates, sources, or connected canvases. It should feel like quiet software made from sheets, frames, comments, sidebars, and reusable blocks.

This recipe is a visual method layer. It does not replace the active Pixflow Design skill, selected design system, craft rules, user brief, screenshots, or Stage A process. Use it to shape the artifact's rhythm, product proof, interface density, typography, and final critique.

## When To Use

- Document, editor, knowledge-base, note-taking, team wiki, collaboration, template-library, research, content workflow, or connected-canvas products.
- Calm SaaS or product pages that need premium clarity without dark-theme drama.
- Pages where the main proof should be a usable editor/workspace shell instead of a generic dashboard.

## When Not To Use

- Highly expressive launches that need loud color, hard shadows, gaming visuals, fashion imagery, or editorial magazine spreads.
- Finance dashboards, social feeds, data-heavy operation centers, or ecommerce grids unless the prompt explicitly asks to translate them into a document workspace.
- Dark-mode-first briefs. Paper defaults to light theme even when the browser or system prefers dark mode. Use dark only for one deliberate contrast panel or footer unless the user asks for dark Paper.

## Signature Rhythm

1. Thin sticky or fixed-feeling top nav on a light paper surface.
2. First viewport with a left or centered hero, large soft-weight headline, short paragraph, two pill CTAs, and a full-width editor/workspace shell near the fold.
3. A document-platform thesis section with a readable text block and three foundation cards.
4. Feature grid with six cards, each carrying a small product UI miniature rather than a generic icon-only benefit.
5. Four-step workflow row that uses large pale numbers, arrows or rules, and compact copy.
6. Use-case section with one large horizontal card plus two smaller cards, each with a believable document/code/campaign preview.
7. Split proof section: headline on one side, vertical advantage list on the other.
8. Pricing or package section with one dark featured plan only when the run needs conversion.
9. Centered final CTA and quiet multi-column footer.

## Visual DNA

- Page background is warm off-white paper, never pure white and never system dark by default.
- Primary surfaces are white cards with thin warm-grey borders and restrained shadows.
- Secondary surfaces are pale document fields used inside mockups, code blocks, search panels, checklist rows, and editor canvases.
- Accent is a soft blue used for primary CTAs, focus states, small dots, active pills, and selected UI rows. Do not turn the whole recipe blue.
- Typography is clean sans with rounded, document-product warmth. Montserrat-style display with Roboto/Inter-style body and PT Mono-style labels works well.
- Hero type should be large but not compressed. At a 1600px viewport, use about 1440px primary content width.
- UI proof should look editable: sidebar, tabs, document title, metadata, inline suggestion, content rows, comment card, permissions, search, or version history.
- Texture is optional and extremely subtle. Prefer layout, rules, borders, and paper panels over visible noise.

## Copy Direction

Use direct product language: documents, workspace, writing, collaboration, comments, drafts, publish, templates, knowledge, search, version history, permissions, AI assistance, teams, sync, review, sources, shared pages.

Write concrete section copy. The page should sound like a real product landing page, not a style demo. Do not mention external recipe sources, internal workflow, Bricks, WordPress, JSON, conversion, or implementation constraints in visible copy.

## Responsive Rules

- Desktop review target is 1600px wide with roughly 1440px primary content width.
- Do not keep a split hero if either column becomes skinny. Center the hero or stack the editor shell if needed.
- On mobile, collapse the editor shell into a simplified document frame with sidebar hidden and no horizontal scroll.
- Feature cards move from three columns to two columns to one column.
- Workflow steps move from four columns to stacked cards with visible numbering.
- Pricing cards stack with the highlighted plan first or second depending on the brief; never let plan buttons overflow.
- Footer columns collapse cleanly and the brand/legal row remains readable.

## Bricks Awareness

Stage A outputs HTML/CSS only. Keep the structure semantic and conversion-friendly:

- Use `section`, `header`, `nav`, `main`, `article`, `aside`, and `footer`.
- Keep pseudo-elements decorative only.
- Build editor mockups from real HTML elements and CSS, not a flat screenshot.
- Use stable grids, aspect ratios, and section backgrounds.
- Avoid fragile absolute positioning for text. Floating suggestion cards are allowed only when they remain non-overlapping at mobile widths.

## Final Critique Questions

- Does the first viewport instantly read as a light document/workspace product?
- Is the editor shell large, readable, and specific enough to be product proof?
- Is the recipe still visible below the hero through document cards, workflow rows, use-case panels, and pricing?
- Are all split layouts wide enough at 1600px, with no accidental sidebar-like paragraphs?
- Does mobile preserve readable document rhythm without horizontal overflow?
